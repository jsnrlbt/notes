## Summary

The author posits that while artificial intelligence is often viewed as a revolutionary force that will "change everything," it is ultimately a new technological tool that must be secured using established information security principles. Rather than treating AI as a mysterious monolith, security professionals should approach it within the context of business functions, recognizing that the core mission—ensuring resources are used only by authorized entities for intended purposes—remains unchanged despite the shift from files and records to models and vectors.

However, AI does introduce distinct challenges that complicate traditional security practices. These include the massive scale of Large Language Models (LLMs), the self-modifying nature of certain algorithms, and the rise of "agentic AI" where autonomous agents take actions on behalf of users. The author emphasizes that areas such as personnel security, access control, and system recoverability require updated strategies, such as linking human identity to agent permissions and prioritizing high availability over traditional backups.

## Key Takeaways

- **Fundamental Principles Persist:** The basic premises of information security do not change; AI systems and their resources must still be restricted to authorized users for authorized purposes.
    
- **Context is Critical:** AI security should be addressed based on its specific application within an enterprise (e.g., robotics in manufacturing vs. text generation in publishing).
    
- **Personnel Security Gaps:** AI development often lacks traditional controls like separation of duties and dual control, making supervision and monitoring of data scientists and engineers vital.
    
- **The Rise of AI Agents:** In agentic systems, security must focus on linking the identity and permission levels of the human user to the autonomous actions taken by the AI agent.
    
- **Recoverability Challenges:** Due to the massive size of LLMs, traditional backups are often impractical; the focus should shift toward resilience and high availability across multiple cloud providers.
    
- **Policy Evolution:** Organizations must define acceptable and unacceptable AI practices, with a clear governance structure to update these policies as the technology rapidly evolves.
    

## Notable Quotes

> "The fact that the information in question is in models and vectors rather than files and records is an irrelevancy."

> "Concepts such as separation of duties and dual control are, thus far, foreign to the field of AI development."

> "As AI systems become a part of the workaday world of business, the importance of the linkage of people and agents will become paramount."

> "In other words, recoverability is not the goal, but rather high availability and resilience."
